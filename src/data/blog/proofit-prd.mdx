    <h2 class="text-2xl sm:text-3xl font-extrabold mb-4">1. The Problem</h2>
    <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">1.1 How Small Businesses Lose Deals Today</h3>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">
        A procurement manager at a 50-person company in Bangalore needs a vendor to print 2,000 branded folders for an office event. She asks a colleague, gets a WhatsApp number for a print shop in Rajajinagar, and messages them.
    </p>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">
        The conversation goes well. The print shop owner seems experienced. The price is right. Then she asks: <strong>"Can you send me your company details? I need to share it with my manager before we confirm."</strong>
    </p>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">
        The print shop owner sends a Canva-made flyer, a Google Photos link with some work samples, and his UPI QR code. She looks at it, feels unsure, and calls the larger print company she used last time — the one that costs 30% more but has a proper website and a GST invoice template ready.
    </p>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">
        The small print shop lost the deal. Not because their work was bad. Because they could not present themselves credibly in that one moment.
    </p>

    <div class="bg-amber-50 border-2 border-amber-500 rounded-2xl p-6 my-6 shadow-[4px_4px_0_0_#d97706]">
        <h4 class="font-bold text-amber-800 text-lg uppercase tracking-wider mb-2">The Core Problem</h4>
        <p class="text-base leading-relaxed text-amber-950">
            Micro and nano businesses in Bangalore do good work and have real clients who trust them. But the moment a new buyer asks for proof — company details, past work, GST, client references — they have nothing organised to show. They lose deals not because of capability but because of credibility.
        </p>
    </div>

    <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">1.2 The Moment the Deal Dies</h3>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">
        This is not a discovery problem. Buyers already find small vendors through colleague referrals, WhatsApp groups, and Justdial. The deal does not die at discovery.
    </p>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">
        It dies at one specific moment — when the buyer asks "send me your company details" and the vendor scrambles. ProofIt is built entirely around that moment. We give vendors a single link they can send in under 10 seconds that answers every question a buyer has before committing to an order.
    </p>

    <h2 class="text-2xl sm:text-3xl font-extrabold mb-4">4. What ProofIt Does</h2>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">

    </p>

    </p>
    
    <blockquote class="my-6 p-4 border-l-4 bg-blue-50 text-blue-900 border-blue-600 quote text-lg font-bold">
        "No app download for buyers. No complex booking platform. No commission on deals. Just proof — sent in one tap, viewed in 30 seconds."
    </blockquote>

    <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">4.1 The Incentive Loop — Why Vendors Will Use It</h3>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">

<div id="vouch" class="my-12 sm:my-16 scroll-mt-24">
    <h2 class="text-2xl sm:text-3xl font-extrabold mb-4">5. The Vouch System</h2>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">
        The vouch system is the trust engine inside ProofIt. It is how a vendor's reputation — which currently lives only inside personal relationships — becomes visible and portable.
    </p>

    <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">5.1 How a Vouch Works</h3>
    <ol class="list-decimal list-inside space-y-2 text-base sm:text-lg my-4">
        <li>Vendor completes a job and receives payment.</li>
        <li>ProofIt sends the vendor a ready-made WhatsApp message to forward to their client: <em>"Hi, it was great working with you. Would you mind sharing a quick review? Takes 30 seconds — [link]"</em></li>
        <li>Client opens the link on their phone. No app download. One question: <em>"Would you work with this vendor again?"</em> — Yes / Maybe / No.</li>
        <li>Optional second screen: <em>"What would you tell a colleague about this vendor?"</em> Free text, capped at 100 characters.</li>
        <li>The vendor's profile updates in real time. Vouch count goes up.</li>
    </ol>

    <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">5.2 Why We Show Numbers, Not Stars</h3>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">
        ProofIt does not show a star rating. Stars are easy to fake, easy to misread, and tell you nothing about volume. Instead we show: <strong>"27 of 31 clients would work with this vendor again."</strong>
    </p>
    <p class="text-base sm:text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">
        This is harder to game. It shows both satisfaction and volume. A buyer reading this knows that 31 real clients have worked with this vendor — that is meaningful social proof on its own, before they even read the percentage.
    </p>

    <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">5.3 Keeping Vouches Honest</h3>
    <ul class="list-disc list-inside space-y-2 text-base sm:text-lg my-4">
        <li><strong>Single-use links:</strong> Vouch links cannot be reused or forwarded.</li>
        <li><strong>Cold-start protections:</strong> Profiles with fewer than 8 vouches show an "Early Profile" label instead of a vouch score. This prevents vendors from sending links to only 3 handpicked clients and showing a perfect score.</li>
        <li><strong>Client-initiated vouches:</strong> Clients can leave a vouch directly from the public profile even without a link from the vendor. This gives unhappy clients a voice.</li>
        <li><strong>Automatic triggers (Phase 2):</strong> Vouch links are triggered automatically after a confirmed UPI payment, removing the cherry-picking problem entirely.</li>
    </ul>
</div>
